Detecting Regulatory Divergence Across Jurisdictions: FDA vs EMA

Introduces cross-jurisdiction regulatory divergence detection as a formal NLP task. Develops a 101-pair expert-annotated benchmark of FDA/EMA requirement pairs and benchmarks four methods — lexical heuristics, NLI cross-encoder, Graph-RAG, and LLM judge — achieving 0.819 Macro-F1 with Claude Haiku.

Problem

Pharmaceutical developers operating across the US and EU must reconcile FDA and EMA guidance documents that frequently diverge — on primary endpoints, safety thresholds, and approval pathways. Manual reconciliation is slow, error-prone, and requires deep regulatory expertise. This paper frames the problem as a formal NLP classification task with three labels:

AGREE
38
Pairs · Requirements align
DIVERGE
40
Pairs · Requirements conflict
SILENT
23
Pairs · One jurisdiction omits

A key finding: SILENT relationships are semantically detectable but invisible to NLI entailment framing — a failure mode that motivated the Graph-RAG and LLM-judge approaches.

Architecture

FDA Guidance Document + EMA Guidance Document │ ▼ Obligation-Level Triplet Extraction (subject · obligation_type · condition · object) e.g. [sponsor, MUST, for UC approval, provide OS data] │ ▼ Graph-RAG Pipeline ├── Build obligation graph per jurisdiction ├── Retrieve semantically similar nodes across corpora └── Construct structured context for LLM │ ▼ LLM Judge (Claude Haiku) Prompt: "Given these two regulatory obligations, classify as AGREE / DIVERGE / SILENT" │ ▼ Classification + Bootstrap CI Evaluation

Dataset

101 requirement pairs drawn from peer-reviewed studies and primary guidance documents across three therapeutic domains: urothelial carcinoma (UC) endpoints, psychiatric disorder treatment guidelines, and radiopharmaceutical dosimetry. All pairs were annotated by domain experts; inter-annotator agreement κ = 0.542 (moderate).

Key Findings

NLI framing is ill-suited for regulatory text. The NLI cross-encoder achieves only 0.271 F1 — well below lexical baselines — because regulatory SILENT relationships have no equivalent in entailment/contradiction/neutral framing.

Obligation-level structure helps but doesn't close the gap. Graph-RAG (0.698) improves substantially over lexical heuristics (0.556) by capturing entity-level obligation structures, but LLM judge (0.819) surpasses it by reasoning holistically over the retrieved context.

LLM judge is the strongest baseline and demonstrates that Claude Haiku can serve as a viable zero-shot regulatory compliance classifier when supplied with structured context.
